General Questions

How accurate are the calculations?

These calculators use standard financial formulas to provide estimates based on the inputs you provide. The results should be considered as projections rather than guarantees as actual returns depend on market performance, which can fluctuate. For SIP calculators, we use compound interest formulas that assume a constant rate of return, which is a simplification of real-world scenarios where returns vary year to year.

Can I save or export my calculation results?

Yes, you can export the calculation summary and amortization result table into a PDF format.

Do these calculators account for taxes?

No, these calculators show pre-tax returns. The actual returns you receive may be subject to capital gains tax, dividend tax, or other applicable taxes depending on your country's tax laws and the investment vehicle you choose. We recommend consulting with a tax advisor to understand the tax implications of your investments.

What return rate should I use for my calculations?

The appropriate return rate depends on your investment choice and risk tolerance:

  • Equity mutual funds: Historically, equity markets have delivered around 10-12% returns over long periods, but with higher volatility.
  • Debt instruments: Usually provide returns in the 6-8% range with lower risk.
  • Balanced/hybrid funds: Typically deliver around 8-10% depending on their asset allocation.

For long-term planning, it's often wise to use conservative estimates (1-2% lower than historical averages) to account for market uncertainties.

What does inflation adjustment mean?

Inflation adjustment accounts for the decrease in purchasing power of money over time. When we show inflation-adjusted values, we're converting future amounts into today's purchasing power, giving you a more realistic idea of what your returns will actually be worth. For example, ₹1 crore after 20 years with 6% annual inflation will have the purchasing power of approximately ₹31 lakhs in today's terms.

SIP Calculator FAQs

What is a SIP and how does it work?

A Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) is an investment method where you invest a fixed amount at regular intervals (typically monthly) into a mutual fund or other investment vehicle. It's similar to a recurring deposit but invested in market-linked instruments. This disciplined approach to investing offers benefits like rupee-cost averaging (buying more units when prices are low) and helps you build wealth gradually without timing the market.

What is the annual step-up feature?

The annual step-up feature allows you to increase your SIP amount by a fixed percentage each year. This is beneficial because:

  • It aligns with your likely income growth over time
  • It helps accelerate your wealth creation
  • It counters the effects of inflation
  • It maximizes the power of compounding

For example, a 10% annual step-up means if you start with ₹5,000 monthly SIP, it becomes ₹5,500 in the second year, ₹6,050 in the third year, and so on.

Is it better to invest a larger amount at once or do a SIP?

Both approaches have their advantages:

SIP Benefits:

  • Reduces risk through rupee cost averaging
  • Suitable for regular income earners
  • Builds investment discipline
  • Lower initial investment requirement

Lump Sum Benefits:

  • Better returns if market is on an upward trend
  • Money works for longer period
  • Suitable for windfall gains or bonus payments

For most investors, a combination approach works best – investing lump sums when available and maintaining regular SIPs.

Lump Sum Calculator FAQs

When should I choose lump sum investment over SIP?

Lump sum investments are generally more suitable in these scenarios:

  • When markets are at a significant low (though timing the market is difficult)
  • When you receive a large sum (bonus, inheritance, etc.) and have no immediate need for it
  • When you have a shorter investment horizon (1-3 years)
  • When you're very confident about the investment and don't need to average out market volatility

For longer time horizons and uncertain market conditions, SIPs typically provide more predictable results.

How does compounding work differently in lump sum vs SIP investments?

In a lump sum investment, your entire principal amount starts compounding immediately. This means every rupee is working for you from day one. This can be advantageous in consistently rising markets.

In a SIP, only a portion of your total investment is compounding from the beginning. Early installments compound for longer periods, while later installments have less time to grow. This results in a time-weighted average return that's typically different from what you'd get with a lump sum.

For example, with a 12% annual return, ₹12 lakhs invested as a lump sum would grow to approximately ₹37.3 lakhs in 10 years. The same amount invested as a ₹10,000 monthly SIP would grow to about ₹23.2 lakhs in the same period.

Goal-based Calculator FAQs

How do I account for inflation in my financial goals?

The goal-based calculator helps you account for inflation by:

  • Allowing you to enter today's cost of your goal (e.g., ₹20 lakhs for a car)
  • Applying an inflation rate to calculate the future cost (e.g., a ₹20 lakh car might cost ₹27 lakhs after 5 years with 6% inflation)
  • Determining the required monthly SIP or lump sum investment to achieve this inflation-adjusted goal

This approach ensures you're saving enough to maintain purchasing power. For long-term goals like retirement or education, accounting for inflation is crucial as prices can double every 12-14 years with 6% inflation.

Can I plan for multiple financial goals simultaneously?

Yes, these goal-based calculator allows you to plan for multiple goals with different time horizons and priorities. For each goal, you can:

  • Specify the current cost and target date
  • Apply different inflation rates based on the goal category (education inflation typically runs higher than general inflation)
  • See the required investment amount for each goal separately
  • Visualize how your investments for different goals will grow over time

Planning multiple goals helps you prioritize and allocate resources efficiently across short-term goals (like vacations), medium-term goals (like home purchase), and long-term goals (like retirement).

EMI Calculator FAQs

How is EMI calculated?

EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) is calculated using this formula:

EMI = [P × R × (1+R)^N]/[(1+R)^N-1]

Where:

  • P is the Principal loan amount
  • R is the monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12 and expressed as a decimal)
  • N is the total number of monthly payments (loan tenure in months)

For example, for a loan of ₹20 lakhs at 8% annual interest for 20 years:

P = 2,000,000; R = 0.08/12 = 0.00667; N = 20 × 12 = 240

EMI = ₹16,731 per month

What is the amortization schedule and how do I interpret it?

The amortization schedule is a complete table showing the breakdown of each EMI payment over the loan tenure. It shows:

  • Interest component: The portion of EMI that goes toward interest
  • Principal component: The portion that reduces your loan balance
  • Outstanding balance: The remaining loan amount after each payment

In the early years of a loan, a larger portion of each EMI goes towards interest. As you progress, more goes toward reducing the principal. Understanding this schedule helps you plan prepayments effectively to reduce overall interest costs.

How do extra payments affect my loan?

Making extra payments beyond your required EMI can significantly reduce your total interest cost and loan tenure. You have two options:

  • Reduce tenure: Keep the same EMI but pay off the loan faster
  • Reduce EMI: Keep the same tenure but lower your monthly payment

For example, on a ₹50 lakh home loan at 8% for 20 years, making an extra payment of ₹5 lakhs in the 5th year could reduce your total interest by approximately ₹11.2 lakhs and shorten your loan by about 2 years and 3 months.

The calculator allows you to model different prepayment scenarios to see their impact.
